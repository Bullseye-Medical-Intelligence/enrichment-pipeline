Field Notes

Your whole list fits.
Few of them buy.

Every targeting tool on the market scores the same thing: clinical fit. Right specialty, right procedures, right patient base. Fit is table stakes. It tells you who could buy. It says nothing about who will. The signal that actually predicts a close is one almost nobody scores.

By Rajiv Kothari, FounderBullseye Medical Intelligence6 min read

Fit is the cheapest signal in the market.

Definitive Healthcare tells you a physician exists. IQVIA tells you the procedure volume. ZoomInfo tells you the title and the email. All of it is fit. All of it is real. And all of it stops at the same place: confirming that an account could be a customer.

Here is the catch. A perfect-fit practice that is hospital-employed, committee-governed, and bound to a slow payer-approval cycle will burn nine months of your rep's calendar and still say no. The clinical match was never the problem. The buying structure was.

Fit predicts the conversation. It does not predict the close. Treat the two as the same thing and you build a pipeline that looks full and converts thin.

The short version

Fit is whether a practice clinically matches your product. Readiness is whether that practice is structured to make a fast buying decision. They are independent. The accounts worth working first are the ones that score on both.

The Wedge
Fit gets you a meeting. Readiness gets you a close.

Readiness is a business-model question, not a clinical one. Is this practice built to move? A practice that already runs cash-pay procedures has done something most practices never do: it has made purchasing decisions without waiting for a payer's permission. It has set its own prices. It has marketed its own services. That muscle is the tell.

The same logic runs through regulatory environment. In states without an insurance mandate for a given category, practices develop self-pay behavior on their own, because nobody is reimbursing them by default. Non-mandate practices buy faster. Not because of geography. Because of how they have learned to operate.

None of this shows up in a procedure-code database. It shows up in how a practice presents itself in public. And that is the part that takes work to read.

Why nobody else scores this.

Fit can be bought off a database in an afternoon. Taxonomy, procedure codes, provider counts. It is a query. Readiness is not a query. It has to be assembled from a practice's public footprint, one signal at a time, then cross-referenced against the specific product you are selling.

That assembly is the work. And the work is exactly why it is a moat. Everyone sells the universe. Almost nobody scores whether the account is built to move.

Bullseye analyzes publicly observable practice-level signals from a physician or practice's online footprint. We do not use patient-level data, claims files, medical records, appointment data, portals, EMRs, private databases, or login-gated systems.

How to read your list differently.

Stop treating clinical fit as the finish line. Use it as the filter it actually is. Once an account clears the fit bar, the ranking question changes entirely: not "does this practice match," but "is this practice built to say yes quickly."

Re-rank the qualified accounts by readiness. Work the practices that score on both dimensions first. That single move concentrates rep time on the accounts most likely to convert, instead of spreading effort evenly across a list where every name looks the same. The reps that already do this by instinct are your best closers. Readiness is just that instinct, made into a signal you can score before the first call.

Common Questions

Fit, readiness, and how to score it.

What is the difference between fit and readiness in physician targeting?
Fit is whether a practice clinically matches your product: the right specialty, procedures, and patient base. Readiness is whether that practice is structured to make a fast buying decision, independent of clinical match. Fit predicts whether a rep gets a meeting. Readiness predicts whether the account closes.
What public signals indicate a practice is ready to buy quickly?
Visible patient financing, cash-pay service lines, an owner-operator structure rather than hospital employment, active advertising of self-pay procedures, and operating in a non-mandate state. Practices already running cash-pay procedures have built the muscle to decide without a payer's permission, which shortens the cycle.
Why do most targeting tools only score clinical fit?
Fit is cheap to score because it pulls straight from a database. Readiness cannot be bought off a list. It has to be assembled signal by signal from a practice's public footprint. That work is why most database vendors stop at fit and leave readiness unscored.
How should a specialty medtech sales leader prioritize a targeting list?
Use clinical fit as a qualifying filter, not a ranking. Then re-rank the qualified accounts by readiness: buying-behavior evidence, business-model structure, and regulatory environment. Work the accounts that score on both first.
